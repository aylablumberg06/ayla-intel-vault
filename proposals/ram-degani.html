Part One · The Honest Opening

You already know how to run a real business.

Most proposals for a new pet brand start with the founder. Yours doesn't — because you’re not most founders. You’ve been running Lone Star Blinds in Dallas for a quarter-century. You know what a real order looks like, what a professional install looks like, what it takes for a first-time customer to become a referral. Your second brand gets to skip the part where it has to prove it’s not a hobbyist.

The question isn’t whether the dog-suspenders venture is a real business — it’s how quickly the rest of the world treats it like one. That means a brand that looks made, not assembled. A site that could sit next to Walkee Paws without flinching. And a sales engine that doesn’t need you to be grinding on Etsy and Instagram fourteen hours a week.

Ram operates Lone Star Blinds on Wix with Kevin handling inbound email. He has 25+ years in window treatments (ASID-affiliated, 5/5 on Houzz). The dog-suspenders venture has no public storefront or socials yet. This proposal is scoped for a brand launch, not a brand refresh.
Part Two · The Three Angles

Which dog
do we sell to?

Before the agents run, we need to pick the market. These are the three places "suspenders for dogs" actually makes money. You’re probably in one of them. The agents are sized for each.

Angle · A
Diaper & senior-dog retention

Heat cycles. Incontinence. Post-surgery. A huge, underserved Etsy market ready to be consolidated by a DTC brand with nicer photos and a better story. Vet-clinic channel is real money. The Dallas-founder-who-actually-knows-fabric angle plays here.

Angle · B
Boot & paw-protection retention

Walkee Paws owns this nationally. In Dallas, you’re fighting the weather question. If you sell, you sell hot pavement in July, not snow in January. Smaller addressable market but higher prestige if you pair with vet referrals.

Angle · C
Novelty & weddings

"Little gentleman" suspenders for dog ring-bearers, photoshoots, Instagram. Low AOV, viral potential, pet boutique channel. A harder business to grow past $300K but a fun beachhead.

The first call is where we pick one. The agent team is configured differently for each — the same six roles, but different channels, pricing, and copy. We don’t build all three at once.

Part Three · The Team

Six agents,
cast from Tennessee Williams.

You chose Cat on a Hot Tin Roof — a Pollitt family affair. So the team is the family. Each one named, each one doing exactly what they do best. Brick hunts. Maggie reads. Big Daddy decides. Mae writes. Gooper audits. Big Mama follows up.

I.

Brick Pollitt — Lead Finder

Finds Dog Owners Who Need You

Scours pet-owner communities, vet directories, breed-specific Facebook groups, local rescue networks, and boutique-retailer lists. Targets by dog breed (the dogs that actually need suspenders), geography, and owner spend signals. Delivers 40–60 qualified prospects a week — ranked, not dumped.

Quiet, steady, never misses a mark
II.

Maggie Pollitt — The Researcher

Reads Every Lead Like a Page

Takes every owner Brick surfaces and writes the one-line pitch. Breed mix, purchase history (Chewy Autoship? Etsy regular? BarkBox subscriber?), Instagram engagement, estimated pet-spend budget. The research Big Daddy needs before he commits to an approach.

Watches everything; misses nothing
III.

Big Daddy Pollitt — The Strategist

Sets the Play — Every Time

Reads Maggie's brief, picks the angle. DTC owner — Instagram DM. Pet boutique — wholesale catalog. Vet office — clinic display program. Rescue org — cause-tied collab. Big Daddy never runs the same play twice in a row. Nothing ships without his sign-off.

The patriarch — final word on every move
IV.

Mae Flynn — The Copywriter

Writes Every Word

Drafts every outbound asset — product descriptions that mention the breed by name, Instagram Reels captions in your voice, wholesale decks for retail buyers, custom pitches for rescues. Mae learns your brand tone and enforces it. No "best value! #1 seller!" nonsense.

The one who makes it sound like you
V.

Gooper Pollitt — Quality Control

The Audit Before Shipment

Reviews every piece of copy, every agent decision, every asset before anything goes out. Gooper’s rule: if this doesn’t sound like a person who actually owns a dog wrote it, it doesn’t ship. The reason the system doesn’t sound like marketing.

The one nobody wants to hear from — until they need him
VI.

Big Mama Pollitt — Outreach & Retention

Sends, Follows Up, Brings Them Back

Runs the sequences. 3-touch cadence, auto-paused on any reply. Then the part every small-brand misses: the post-purchase follow-up that turns a one-pair buyer into a referral source. New-puppy reminder six months later. Review-prompt the week after delivery. The loop that makes a niche business profitable.

Fills the house; won’t let anyone leave hungry
